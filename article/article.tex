\begin{abstract}
    Wills conjectured that, for any integers $u_1,\ldots,u_k$, 
    there is a real number $t$ such that, for all $i=1,\ldots,k$, \[\norm{tu_i}\geq\frac{1}{k+1},\] 
    where $\norm{x}$ is the distance from $x$ to the closest integer. This statement is known as the Lonely Runner Conjecture.
    A computational method developed by Rosenfeld and the second author verified the conjecture for $k\leq9$.
    We further refine this method with new sieving techniques and employ a polynomial technique to show that
    any $(u_1,\ldots,u_k)\equiv\onetok\pmod{p}$ with $\gcd(u_1,\ldots,u_k)=1$ satisfies the conjecture for $k+1$ and $p > k^2+k$ 
    are both odd primes. Ultimately, we provide a computer-assisted proof of the Lonely Runner Conjecture for $k\in\{10,11,12\}$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The Lonely Runner Conjecture arose in the study of Diophantine approximation \cite{Wills},
though it has been reinterpreted in the context of view obstruction and billiard trajectories 
\cite{cusick1984view}, covering radii of zonotopes \cite{henze_covering_2017}, and the chromatic number of a 
unit-distance graph \cite{zhu2002circular}. 
It was conjectured independently by Wills \cite{Wills} in 1967 and Cusick \cite{cusick1984view} in 1984. 
By writing $\norm{x}=\min(x-\lfloor x\rfloor, \lceil x\rceil-x)$, the distance from $x$ to the closest integer, 
the conjecture can be stated as follows:

\begin{conjecture} \label{lonelyconj}
  Let $u_1,\ldots,u_{k+1}$ be distinct real numbers. For each $i \in\{1,\ldots,k+1\}$, there is a real number $t$ such that
  \[ \norm{t u_i-tu_j} \ge \frac{1}{k+1} \quad \text{for all } j \ne i. \]
\end{conjecture}

Conjecture \ref{lonelyconj} has a popular interpretation that gives its name: if $k+1$ runners run with distinct,
constant speeds around a unit-length track, each of them will be \emph{lonely} at some time. 
Being lonely means being at least $\tfrac{1}{k+1}$ away from other runners. 
However, most advances work with one fewer runner and only positive integer speeds, 
possible due to \cite{Bohman} and \cite{henze_covering_2017} that showed the equivalence between Conjecture \ref{lonelyconj}
and the following statement:

\begin{conjecture} \label{lonelyconjred}
    All $(u_1,\ldots,u_k)\in\Z_{>0}^k$ have the \emph{LR property}, meaning that there is a real number $t$ such that
    \[\norm{tu_i}\geq\frac{1}{k+1} \quad \text{for all } i = 1,\dots,k.\] 
    The time $t$ where the above inequalities hold is called a \emph{witness} of the LR property.
    
    We denote this statement by $LRC(k)$ or, as customary, the Lonely Runner Conjecture for $k+1$ runners.
\end{conjecture}

Conjecture \ref{lonelyconjred} is known to hold if we replace $\frac{1}{k+1}$ with
$\frac{1}{2k}+\frac{1}{k^{5/3+o(1)}}$ \cite{bedert2025riesz}, or, more closely related to our approach,
for $k\leq9$ (at most 10 runners). The two smallest values of $k$ are trivial. 
Betke~and~Wills~\cite{fourrunners} first proved the case $k=3$, Cusick~and~Pomerance~\cite{cusick1984view} proved $k=4$, 
and Bohman,~Holzman,~and~Kleitman~\cite{Bohman} proved $k=5$. Barajas~and~Serra~\cite{barajas_lonely_2008} 
settled the case $k=6$ in 2008.

This remained the largest verified case until September 2025, when Rosenfeld~\cite{Rosenfeld} introduced a new 
framework to handle $k=7$ (and later $k=8$ \cite{rosenfeld2025lonely2}). 
He demonstrated that, with computer assistance, the conjecture can be verified in a number of small ansatze.
In doing so, he relied on the finite-checking result of Malikiosis, Santos, and Schymura~\cite{Malikiosis}, 
which follows from a similar result of Giri~and~Kravitz~\cite{giri_structure_2026}. 
The~second~author~\cite{Trakulthongchai} then, in November 2025, 
presented a sieving scheme that substantially reduces the work for verification, thereby establishing the proof for $k\in\{8,9\}$. 

The main objective of this paper is to prove $LRC(k)$ for $k\in\{10,11,12\}$.
\begin{theorem} \label{thm:main}
    $LRC(k)$ holds for $k\leq12$.
\end{theorem}

In order to carry out the proof, we improve on the framework of \cite{Rosenfeld} and \cite{Trakulthongchai}
both mathematically and computationally. We refine the sieving scheme with extra intermediate sieves and exploit a modulo $p$ symmetry 
during implementation to reduce the necessary verifications. 
These improvements suffice, though only marginally, to conclude the conjecture for $k \in \set{10, 11}$. 
To strengthen our result, we employ a polynomial method to rule out $\onetok$ as a possible counterexample speed tuple,
which significantly speedups the prove for $k=10$ and settles the proof for $k=12$.

Our proof strategy is outlined in the next section. 
Section~\ref{sec:sieves} discusses the intermediate sieves technique and 
Section~\ref{sec:onetok} is devoted to proving Lemma~\ref{lem:onetokintro}. 
We describe the verification algorithms and their implementation in Section~\ref{sec:algo}, 
the results of which establish Theorem~\ref{thm:main} in Section~\ref{sec:result}.
Lastly, we discuss in Section~\ref{sec:further} how this method may be further extended. 

We adopt the following choices for notation. We write $aS=\{as:s\in S\}$ for $a\in\R$ and $S\subseteq\R$. 
The additive group modulo $n$ is denoted $\Int{n}$, the multiplicative group modulo $n$ is $\Unit{n}$, and 
\[\Int{p, l}:=\Int{pl}\setminus p\Int{l}.\]
Let $\pi_{m \to n}:\Int{m} \to \Int{n}$ denote the natural projection map when $n\mid m$
and $\pi_n: \Z \to \Int{n}$ denote the projection modulo $n$ of integers.
The fractional part of $x\in\R$ is denoted by $\fracpart{x}$.

We use boldface letters for vectors, subscripts to indicate coordinates of a vector, 
and $\widehat{\cdot}$ to indicate the omission of a certain coordinate in a vector. 
For example, $u_i$ is the $i^\text{th}$ coordinate of the vector $\ubf$.
We refer to a vector $\ubf$ or $\vbf$ as a \emph{speed tuple} to match the conjecture's settings. 
The application of a scalar function to vectors is coordinate-wise. 

\section{Proof strategy}

Rosenfeld's \cite{Rosenfeld} key insight is that if $\ubf\equiv\vbf\pmod{lp}$, 
then $\norm{tv_i}=\norm{tu_i}$ for every $i$ whenever $t$ is a rational with denominator $lp$. 
In particular, if we know that $\vbf\in\Int{lp}^k$ has a witness time in the form $t\in\frac{1}{lp}\Z$,
then every $\ubf\in\pi^{-1}_{lp}(\vbf)$ has the same witness time and thus the LR property.
If every $\vbf\in\Int{p,l}^k$ has a witness time in the form $t\in\frac{1}{lp}\Z$,
then one of the speeds in a counterexample to $LRC(k)$ must be divisible by $p$.
The following definitions and lemmas summarize what must be verified.

\begin{definition}[Definition 3.1, \cite{Trakulthongchai}]\label{def:improper}
    Let $p$ be prime, $k \geq 2$ and $l$ be positive integers.
    A speed tuple $\vbf\in\Int{p,l}^k$ is $(k,p,l)$-\emph{proper} if one of the following conditions holds: 
  \begin{itemize}
    \item there exists $i$ such that $\gcd(l, v_1, \dots, \widehat{v_{i}}, \dots, v_k) > 1$.
    \item there exists $t \in\frac{1}{lp}\Z$ such that $\norm{tv_i} \geq \frac{1}{k+1}$ for all $i$.
  \end{itemize}
   If $\vbf$ is not proper, it is \emph{improper}. Moreover, let $I(k,p,l)$ denote the set of all improper speed tuples.
\end{definition}

A minor but important deviation from the definition in \cite{Trakulthongchai} 
is that the first condition (the gcd condition) involves $l$ rather than $lp$.
This definition aligns with the one given in \cite{Trakulthongchai} as $p$ is chosen to be prime in this work.

\begin{lemma}[Lemma 3.3, \cite{Trakulthongchai}] \label{lem:divp}
  % k-1 enough?
    For $k\geq3$, if $LRC(k-1)$ is true and $I(k,p,l)$ is empty for some $l$, then any counterexample
    $\ubf$ to $LRC(k)$ satisfies $p \mid u_1 \cdots u_k$. 
\end{lemma}

Lemma~\ref{lem:divp} translates proving Conjecture \ref{lonelyconjred} to the computer verification task
of showiung $I(k,p,l_p)=\emptyset$ for some $l_p$, for many primes $p$.
Since the choice of $l$ is completely irrelevant to the final conclusion, 
we introduce a new terminology to simplify our discussion and implementation. 
\begin{definition}
  \label{def:eventualproper}
  Let $k \geq 2$ be an integer and $p$ be a prime. 
  A speed tuple $\vbf \in \Int{p,1}^k$ is said to be \emph{eventually $(k,p)$-proper}
  if there is $l$ such 
  that \[ \vbf \notin \pi_{lp \to p} I(k, p, l). \]
  Let $J(k,p)$ denote the set of all $\vbf \in \Z_{p,1}^k$ that are not eventually $(k,p)$-proper.
\end{definition}

Eventual properness might appear to be a local property of each speed tuple $\vbf$,
but having this local properties throughout implies the global conclusion that $I(k,p,l)=\emptyset$ for some $l$.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:evenprop}
    For fixed $k$ and $p$, $J(k,p)=\emptyset$ if and only if $I(k,p,l)=\emptyset$ for some $l$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    The backward direction is immediate from the definition. 
    To prove the forward direction, note that $J(k,p)=\emptyset$ implies that,
    for each $\vbf\in \Int{p,1}^k$, there is $l_\vbf$ for which $\vbf \notin \pi_{l_\vbf p \to p} I(k, p, l_\vbf)$. 
    Then pick \[l=\operatorname{lcm}\{l_\vbf:\vbf\in\Int{p,1}^k\}.\] 
    
    Fix $\ubf\in\Int{p,l}^k$. 
    Let $\vbf=\pi_{lp\rightarrow p}\ubf$ and $\vbf'=\pi_{lp\rightarrow l_\vbf p}\ubf$,
    so we can write \[\ubf=(a_1l_\vbf p+v_1',\ldots,a_kl_\vbf p+v_k')\] for some integers $a_1,\ldots,a_k$. 
    
    Notice that $\pi_{l_{\vbf}p \to p} \vbf' = \vbf$. 
    By definition of $l_\vbf$, $\vbf'$ is $(k,p,l_\vbf)$-proper. 
    If $\vbf'$ satisfies the gcd condition,
    then there is $i$ such that there is $d:=\gcd(l_\vbf, v'_1, \dots, \widehat{v'_{i}}, \dots, v'_k) > 1$. 
    It follows that $d\mid l$ and $d\mid u_j$ for all $j\neq i$, hence $\ubf$ also satisfies the gcd condition.

    Otherwise, there is $t\in\frac{1}{l_\vbf p}\Z$ such that $\norm{tv'_i}\geq\frac{1}{k+1}$ for all $i$. 
    Then observe that $\frac{1}{l_\vbf p}\Z\subseteq\frac{1}{lp}\Z$, so $t\in\frac{1}{lp}\Z$,
    and $\norm{tu_i}=\norm{tv_i}\geq\frac{1}{k+1}$ for all $i$.

    In both cases, we have shown $\ubf$ to be $(k,p,l)$-proper.
    Therefore, $I(k,p,l)=\emptyset$.
\end{proof}

As a consequence, the emptiness of $J(k,p)$ and $I(k,p,l)$ at some $l$ may be discussed interchangeably.
We may also restate Lemma~\ref{lem:divp} in a more usable form for the proof of our main Theorem \ref{thm:main}.
\begin{corollary}\label{cor:evenpropdivp}
  %TODO k-1 enough?
  Let $k \ge 3$ and assume that $LRC(k-1)$ is true.
  If $J(k,p)$ is empty, then any counterexample $\ubf$ to $LRC(k)$ has $p \mid u_1\cdots u_k$.
\end{corollary}

We also require an upper bound on the runners' speeds beyond which the LR property is guaranteed.
The best such bound at present was obtained by Malikiosis,~Santos,~and~Schymura~\cite{Malikiosis}:
\begin{lemma}[Corollary 3, \cite{Rosenfeld}] \label{lem:proddivB}
    If $LRC(k-1)$ holds, then any counterexample $\ubf$ to $LRC(k)$ must have
    \[ u_1\cdots u_k < B_k \quad \text{where} \quad B_k := \paren{\frac{\binom{k+1}{2}^{k-1}}{k}}^k. \]
\end{lemma}

Therefore, if we satisfy the precondition of Corollary \ref{cor:evenpropdivp} for a large number of primes,
we can obtain a lower bound of the speeds in any counterexamples. 
Together with stated upper bound, we can conclude the absence of counterexample.

The final ingredient needed to handle $k \in \set{10,12}$ is Lemma~\ref{lem:onetokintro}, 
which shows that $\onetok$ must be eventually $(k, p)$-proper for a sufficiently large $p$.
This statement is proved in Section~\ref{sec:onetok}

\begin{lemma} \label{lem:onetokintro}
    Let $k+1$ and $p>k^2+k$ be odd primes. $\onetok \not\in J(k, p)$
\end{lemma}

Finally, we consolidate the verification task into the following proposition,
which will be used to conclude the main theorem
\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:verify}
  % TODO k - 1 enough?
  Let $k \ge 3$ and $P$ be a set of primes such that $J(k,p)=\emptyset$ for all $p\in P$. 
  If $LRC(k-1)$ holds and
  \[\prod_{p \in P} p\geq B_k.\] 
  Then, $LRC(k)$ is true.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
    Assume that there exists a counterexample $\ubf$ to $LRC(k)$. 
    By Corollary \ref{cor:evenpropdivp}, $\prod_{p\in P}p$ divides $u_1\cdots u_k$ and so 
    \[u_1\cdots u_k\geq \prod_{p \in P} p\geq B_k>u_1\cdots u_k,\] 
    where the last inequality comes from Lemma \ref{lem:proddivB}. 
    This is a contradiction.
\end{proof}

\section{Sieving techniques} \label{sec:sieves}
Recall that our strategy relies on verifying that $J(k,p)=\emptyset$. 
The first approach is to check all tuples in $\Z^{k}_{pl}$ to verify that $I(k,p,l)=\emptyset$,
but this entails checking approximately $(pl)^k$ tuples. 
Moreover, for reasons to be given in Remark~\ref{rem:tightset},
we need $l\geq k+1$, so this approach becomes very laborious even for $k=8$ \cite{rosenfeld2025lonely2}.

Before motivating them, we formalize and validate the two techniques that allows for efficient verification 
in the following proposition.
\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:liftproj}
    Let $S\subseteq\Int{p,l}^k$ be such that $J(k,p)\subseteq\pi_{p}S$. 
    We also have $J(k,p)\subseteq\pi_pS'$ if $S'$ is constructed in one of the following ways:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item (lifting with $c$) $S'=\{\vbf\in\pi_{clp\rightarrow lp}^{-1}S:\vbf\text{ is $(k,p,cl)$-improper}\}$
        \item (projecting) $S'=\pi_pS$.
    \end{itemize}
    \begin{proof}
        The second case is obvious. In the first case, note that
        \[\begin{split}
            \pi_pS'&=\pi_p\paren{\pi_{clp\rightarrow lp}^{-1}S\cap I(k,p,cl)} \\
                   &=\pi_p\pi_{clp\rightarrow lp}^{-1}S\cap\pi_pI(k,p,cl) \\
                   &=\pi_pS\cap\pi_pI(k,p,cl).
        \end{split}
        \] As $J(k,p)$ is a subset of $\pi_pS$ by assumption and a subset of $\pi_pI(k,p,cl)$ by definition, 
        it is also a subset of $\pi_pS'$.
        \footnote{This proof may look deceptively simple, and perhaps curiously, 
          does not utilize the lifting lemma (Lemma 3.5 \cite{Trakulthongchai}) anywhere. 
          This is because the real work (and the very same work as the lifting lemma) has been done 
          in Lemma~\ref{lem:evenprop} to show that 
          $J(k,p)=\emptyset$ is indeed the same as $I(k,p,l)=\emptyset$ for some $l$. 
          Without this fact, there is no divisibility result in Corollary~\ref{cor:evenpropdivp} 
          hence determining $J(k,p)$ is pointless.
        }
    \end{proof}
\end{proposition}

The two actions are lifting, which we generalize from \cite{Trakulthongchai}, and backward projection, a new technique.
Proposition~\ref{prop:liftproj} ensures that both actions preserve the inclusion of $J(k,p)$ (under projection), 
which means that we cannot lose a non-eventually proper speed tuple in the process. 
Now, we will motivate each of them by their applications.

\subsection{Lifting}
\label{subsec:efflift}
Lifting is the main innovation of \cite{Trakulthongchai}. 
The process is described in the construction of $S'$ in Proposition~\ref{prop:liftproj}: suppose we have a tuple in $\Z^k_{pl}$,
we can \emph{lift} it to the preimage in $\Z^k_{pcl}$, and then discard all proper speed tuples.

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:tightset}
    The speed tuple $\onetok$ is lonely precisely when $t=\frac{s}{k+1}, \gcd(s,k+1)=1$. 
    Therefore, if $p>k+1$ and $k+1\nmid l$, then there is no such time in $\frac{1}{pl}\Z$,
    so $\onetok\in I(k,p,l)$. 
    In other words, if $p>k+1$, then $I(k,p,l)=\emptyset$ only if $k+1\mid l$. 
    As a consequence, if $k+1$ is prime and we start with tuples in $\Z^k_{p}$
    we need to lift with $k+1$ (or a multiple of $k+1$, but this would be unoptimal)
    at least once because we can only eliminate $\onetok$ only once we get to $\Z^{k}_{p(k+1)}$
\end{remark}

Even though it is impossible that $I(k,p,1)=\emptyset$, in view of applying Proposition~\ref{prop:liftproj}, 
we may still compute $I(k,p,1)$ first.
This is because $I(k, p, 1)$ is an \emph{upper bound}, a superset, of $J(k, p)$ 
that is easy enough to compute as it only requires checking $O(p^k)$ speed tuples.
After obtaining that, we can lift with a divisor of $k+1$ to obtain a better upper bound.

Observe that lifting a set $S$ of tuples with a multiplier $c$ requires checking
\[ O\left( \abs{ \pi^{-1}_{clp \to lp} S }\right) = O(c^k\cdot|S|) \]
speed tuples.
As $S$ is empirically found to be smaller as we lift
\footnote{
  Even though lifting increases the number of speed tuples to be checked,
  it seems that the increased precision of the allowed time in the lifted ansatz has a greater effect \cite{Trakulthongchai}.
}, we should take $c$ to be small at first. 
Furthermore, as $k$ gets larger, the value of $c$ has greater effect on the computational cost, 
and the $c=2$ lift becomes comparatively cheaper
\footnote{
  For example, when $k=11$, lifting with $c=5$ is $(5/2)^11$, approximately $2 \times 10^4$ times more computationally expensive than lifting with $c=2$.
}.

The key improvement from \cite{Trakulthongchai} is noting that, in fact, we can take this idea to the extreme.
Since the $c=2$ lift is computationally cheaper to compute, 
we can apply a $c=2$ lift first (and successively) whether or not $2$ is a prime factor of $k$.
This is done to trim down the number of speed tuples in $S$ when we apply a more computationally expensive, but necessary lift.
Even better, doing this will never increase the number of speed tuples at the end because we can always project backward,
as we explain in the next subsection.
 
\subsection{Backward projection}
\label{subsec:backproj}
Backward projection is just the application of $\pi_p$ to pull back the speed tuples back to $\Z^k_{p}$
Just as lifting can reduce the size of the search space, projection achieves a similar effect. 
This is due to the fact that for any set $S$,
\[\abs{\pi_p S}\leq\abs{S}.\]
The only time projecting becomes counterproductive is if we lift afterwards with the same constant $c$ that was previously used,
because then we are intersecting with the same improper speed tuple and hence no speed tuples can be further eliminated.

Therefore, in general, we can lift with $c=2$ successively, then project back, 
then lift with $c=3$ successively and project back, and so on. 
This is the strategy that we use in our verification algorithms to be described in Subsection~\ref{subsec:algo}.

\section{Eventual properness of
  \texorpdfstring{$(1, 2,\ldots, k)$}{(1, 2, ..., k)}
} \label{sec:onetok}
According to Remark~\ref{rem:tightset}, when $k+1$ is prime, we have to lift with the multiple $c=k+1$. 
This lift is extremely computationally expensive: for $k=12$, the $c=k+1$ lift is $(13/2)^12$, approximately $5\cdot10^9$ 
times more computationally expensive than the $c=2$ lift.
If we are to verify the conjecture for $k\in\{10,12\}$ in a reasonable time, we need to avoid doing this lift computationally.
For many primes $p$, after lifting $I(10,p,1)$ with $c=2,3$ several times and projecting back,
the only tuples that cannot be shown eventually proper are equivalents
\footnote{
  Speed tuples that are eventually proper if and only if $\onetok$ is,
  to be made explicit in Subsection~\ref{subsec:equiv}.
} 
of $( 1, 2, \ldots, 10 )$. 
The analogous observation holds when $k=12$.

Therefore, the aim of this section is to determine the eventual properness
\footnote{
  We may frame the results in terms of $(k,p,k+1)$-properness,
  but instead we use eventual $(k,p)$-properness for the sake of continuity.
},
of $\onetok$. 
We emphasize that our method still requires \emph{lifting} with $c=k+1$.
That is, we do not aim to skip the lifting but to establish properness of speed tuples in 
$\pi^{-1}_{(k+1)p\rightarrow p}\onetok$ analytically rather than computationally.

First, we state and prove Proposition~\ref{prop:nullstellensatz} which will be the main ingredient used in this section.
For convenience, we write 
\[ N_{k} := \set{ \vbf \ne \origin \text{ and } \vbf \text{ has at least one zero coordinate } : \vbf \in \Z_{k+1}^k } \].
\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:nullstellensatz}
    Let $k+1$ be an odd prime. For all vectors $\vbf \in N_k$,
    there is $s$ and $r$ in $\Unit{k+1}$ such that
    \[s\vbf + r\onetok  \in  \{1,\ldots,k-1\}^k\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
    In this proof, we work in the field $\Int{k+1}$.
    Define \[V_m = \set{v_i+mi:i\in \set{1,\ldots,k} }\] for $m \in \Unit{k+1}$.
    We say $m\in \Unit{k+1}$ is \emph{good} if $0 \notin V_m$ and \emph{bad} otherwise. 
    Let $G$ be the set of good $m$'s.

    Now, as $\vbf$ contains at least one zero coordinate, it contains at most $k-1$ non-zero coordinates.
    For each non-zero coordinate $v_i$, there is exactly one choice of $m\in\Unit{k+1}$ such that $v_i + m i = 0$, 
    whereas for each zero coordinate, there cannot be such $m$. 
    Thus, there are at most $k-1$ bad units.
    This implies that $\abs{G} \ge 1$.

    We continue the proof by contraposition. Assume that for all $r$ and $s$, 
    \[s\vbf + r\onetok  \notin  \{1,\ldots,k-1\}^k\] then we will show that $\vbf = 0$.

    Let $m$ be good and $s\in\Unit{k+1}$. Then $r:=sm\in\Unit{k+1}$ and so, by assumption,
    \[ s\vbf + r\onetok \not\in \set{1, \dots, k-1}^k. \]
    Thus, there is an index $i$ such that $s (v_i + m i) \in \set{0, -1}$.
    As $m$ is good and $s\neq0$, it is impossible for the product to be $0$, and so it must be $-1$. Hence, 
    \[ v_i + m i = -s^{-1}. \]
    As $s$ was chosen arbitrarily, $V_m = \Unit{k+1}$.

    From $V_m = \Unit{k+1}$ when $m$ is good, we have that
    \[k! = \prod_{i=1}^k(v_i+mi) = \prod_{i=1}^k i \cdot \prod_{i=1}^k (v_i i^{-1} + m) = k!\prod_{i=1}^k (v_i i^{-1}+m),\]
    which gives \[\prod_{i=1}^k (v_i i^{-1} + m) = 1.\]
    On the other hand, when $m$ is bad, then there is an index $i$ such that $v_i + m i = 0$, hence 
    \[ \prod_{i=1}^k (v_i i^{-1} + m) = \prod_{i=1}^k (v_i + m i) i^{-1} = 0. \]
    Therefore, the polynomial \[ P(X) := \prod_{i=1}^k (v_ii^{-1}+X) \] is an indicator function of $G$ on $\Unit{k+1}$.
    Note also that since $\vbf$ contains at least one zero coordinate, $P(0) = 0$.

    As $k+1$ is prime, Fermat's Little Theorem gives another indicator function of $G$, which is
    \[ Q(X) := \sum_{m \in G} (1-(X-m)^k), \] 
    where the sum is not empty as $\abs{G} \ge 1$.
    It also follows that $Q$ is a polynomial of degree $k$.
    
    As $P(0) = 0 = Q(0)$, $P$ and $Q$ are polynomials of degree at most $k$ that agree on $k+1$ points, $P = Q$.
    In particular, their leading coefficients must be equal, that is 
    $1 \equiv -\abs{G} \pmod{k+1}$. Since $\abs{G} \le k$ as integers, we must have that $\abs{G} = k$.
    Therefore, there is no bad $m$. Specifically, for any $i\in\{1,\ldots,k\}$, $v_i+mi \neq 0$
    for all $m\in\Unit{p}$. 
    However, this implies $v_i = 0$ for all $i$, that is, $\vbf = \mathbf{0}$.
\end{proof}

The strategy we will later use to prove Lemma~\ref{lem:corlarge} is that we want to decouple and limit dependency of the prime $p$. 
Instead of considering times of the form $t=\frac{a}{(k+1)p}$ we decompose them into the partial fractions $t=\frac{s}{k+1}+\frac{r}{p}$.
The former term has no dependence of $p$ and to limit the effect of $p$ of the latter term, we define 
\[ \rbf_k(t)=\left( \floor{ (k+1) \fracpart{t}}, \; \floor{ (k+1)\fracpart{2t} }, \; \ldots, \; \floor{ (k+1) \fracpart{kt}} \right) \] 
for $t\in\R$.
Recall that we are focusing in the settings of $k+1$ runners with speed $0, 1, \dots, k$.
Intuitively, $\rbf_k(t)_i = x$ means that the runner with speed $i$, will be in the range 
$\left[ \frac{x}{k+1}, \frac{x+1}{k+1} \right)$ of the unit circle.
With this approximate location, we state Lemma~\ref{lem:resolvekplusone}.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:resolvekplusone}
    Let $k+1$ be an odd prime. For every $\vbf \in N_k$, there is $s \in \Zko$ and $r \in \Z$ for which 
    \[ s\vbf + \rbf_k(\tfrac r{k+1}) \in \{1,\ldots,k-1\}^k\] when considered modulo $k+1$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  Let $\vbf$ be any speed tuple in $\Zko^k\setminus\{\origin\}$ that contains a zero coordinate.
  By Proposition~\ref{prop:nullstellensatz},
  there exist $s,r\in\Unit{k+1}\subseteq\Int{k+1}$ such that
  \[s\vbf + r\onetok  \in \{1,\ldots,k-1\}^k.\] 
  By definition, \[\begin{split}
      \rbf_k\left(\tfrac{r}{k+1}\right) 
      &=\left(\floor*{ (k+1)\fracpart*{\tfrac{r}{k+1}} },\floor*{ (k+1)\fracpart*{\tfrac{2r}{k+1}} },\ldots, 
        \floor*{ (k+1)\fracpart*{\tfrac{kr}{k+1}}} \right)\\
      &=(r,2r,\ldots,kr)\\&=r\onetok
  \end{split}\]
  modulo $k+1$. Therefore,
  \[s\vbf+\rbf_k \paren{\tfrac{r}{k+1}} \in \{1,\ldots,k-1\}^k,\]
\end{proof}

A corollary of Lemma~\ref{lem:resolvekplusone} is that $\onetok$ is eventually $(k,k+1)$-proper, but this is trivial.
In fact, we only need the condition  
\[s\vbf + r\onetok  \notin  \{1,\ldots,k\}^k.\] 
to show eventual $(k, p)$-properness of $\onetok$.
However, Having a stronger condition of 
\[s\vbf + r\onetok  \notin  \{1,\ldots,k-1\}^k\]
from Proposition~\ref{prop:nullstellensatz} allows the extension of the result to larger primes via the next lemma.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:resolvep}
    Let $k+1$ and $p > k(k+1)$ be odd primes. For every $\vbf \in N_k$,
    there is $s \in \Zko$ and $r \in \Z$ for which 
    \[ s\vbf + \rbf_k(r/p) \in \{1,\ldots,k-1\}^k\] when considered modulo $k+1$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  From Lemma~\ref{lem:resolvekplusone} we have got that there is $s \in \Zko$ and $r \in \Z$ for which
    \[ s\vbf + \rbf_k(r/(k+1)) \in \{1,\ldots,k-1\}^k\]
  Thus, it suffices to show that $\rbf_k(\tfrac{1}{k+1} \Z) \subseteq \rbf_k(\tfrac{1}{p} \Z)$ for all $p \ge k(k+1)$.

  Fix $p$ to be a prime greater than $k(k+1)$.

  Notice that $\rbf_k$ is a piecewise constant function. Moreover, 
  $x$ is a discontinuity of $\rbf_k$ if and only if it is a discontinuity of $R_{k+1}(ix)$ for some $i$.
  Those points of discontinuity are precisely points of the form  $\frac{a}{i(k+1)}$ for integer $a$ and $1 \le i \le k$.

  Choose an integer $n$ and denote $x := \tfrac{n}{k+1}$. Observe that $x$ is a discontinuity of $\rbf_k$ and let $y$ be 
  the smallest number greater than $x$ that is also a discontinuity of $\rbf_k$. We know that $y$ can be written as 
  $\tfrac{a}{i(k+1)}$ for some integer $a$ and $1 \le i \le k$, so it follows that 
  \[ y - x = \frac{a}{i(k+1)} - \frac{n}{k+1} = \frac{a - ni}{i(k+1)} \ge \frac{1}{i(k+1)} \ge \frac{1}{k(k+1)}. \]

  As $y$ was chosen to be the smallest such discontinuity, $\rbf_k$ is constant on the interval $[x, y)$. 
  Since the interval's width is at least $\tfrac{1}{k(k+1)} \ge \tfrac{1}{p}$, 
  there must be an integer $m$ such that $\tfrac{m}{p} \in [x, y)$.
  For such value $m$, $\rbf_k (\frac{n}{k+1}) = \rbf_k (\frac{m}{p})$.
  As $n$ is arbitrary, the desired inclusion follows.
\end{proof}

With this lemma, we finally obtain the headline result that assures eventual properness of $\onetok$ for $p \geq k(k+1)$.

\begin{lemma}[Restatement of Lemma~\ref{lem:onetokintro}]
    \label{lem:corlarge}
    Let $k+1$ be an odd prime. Then, $\onetok$ is eventually $(k,p)$-proper for all primes $p \geq k(k+1)$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    We will show that every $\ubf \in \pi_{p(k+1) \to p}^{-1}\onetok$ is $(k,p,k+1)$-proper, 
    and hence $\onetok$ is eventually $(k,p)$-proper.
    Let $u_i = a_ip+i$ for some $a_i\in\Int{k+1}$ for $i=1,\ldots,k$.
    By considering a time of the form $t=\frac{s}{k+1}+\frac{r}{p}$ for $s\in\Z$ and $r\in\Z$, 
    note that the fractional part of $tu_i$ is given by
    \[ \fracpart{tu_i}=\fracpart*{\left(\frac{s}{k+1}+\frac{r}{p}\right)(a_ip+i)}=\fracpart*{\frac{su'_i}{k+1}+\frac{ri}{p}} \] 
    where $\ubf' := \pi_{p(k+1)\to k+1} \ubf$. 
    
    If $\ubf'=\origin$, then it follows that $k+1\mid\gcd(\ubf)$, thus \[k+1\mid\gcd(k+1,\ubf)\]
    hence $\ubf$ is $(k,p,k+1)$-proper because it satisfies the gcd condition.

    If $\ubf'$ contains no zero coordinate, then pick $r=0$ and $s=1$ and we have 
    \[ \fracpart{tu_i}=\fracpart*{\frac{u'_i}{k+1}} \]
    Since $u_i \ne 0$, $\norm{tu_i} \ge \frac{1}{k+1}$ for all $i$.

    Otherwise, by Lemma~\ref{lem:resolvep} there is a choice of $r$ and $s$ such that 
    \[ s\ubf' + \rbf_k(r/p) \in \{1,\ldots,k-1\}^k \]
    when considered modulo $k+1$. 
    This means that for each coordinate $i$, there are $A_i$ and $B_i$ such that 
    $\fracpart{\frac{sv_i}{k+1}}=\frac{A_i}{k+1}$ and 
    $\fracpart{\frac{ri}{p}}\in\left[\frac{B_i}{k+1},\frac{B_i+1}{k+1}\right)$ 
    where $C_i:=A_i+B_i$ is in $\set{1,\ldots,k-1}$ when considered modulo $k+1$. 
    Therefore,
    \[\fracpart{tu_i}=\fracpart*{\frac{su_i}{k+1}+\frac{ri}{p}}\in\left[\frac{C_i}{k+1},\frac{C_i+1}{k+1}\right)\]
    and in particular the set on the right-hand side does not intersect with $[0,\frac{1}{k+1}) \cup (\frac{k}{k+1},1)$.  
    Since this holds for every $i$, $\ubf$ has the LR property.

    As there exists a witness time $t=\frac{s}{k+1}+\frac{r}{p}\in\frac{1}{p(k+1)}\Z$, $\ubf$ is $(k,p,k+1)$-proper.
\end{proof}

\section{Implementation}
\label{sec:algo}

\subsection{Equivalence of speed tuples}
\label{subsec:equiv}
It is clear that some speed tuples in an ansatz are essentially the same, so we only need to verify properness for one of them.
We say that two speed tuples $\ubf$ and $\vbf$ in $\Int{p,1}^k$ are \emph{equivalent} if $\vbf$ can be obtained from $\ubf$ 
by performing a finite number of the following actions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Permuting the coordinates of $\ubf$
    \item Flipping the sign of a coordinate $u_i$ to $-u_i$
    \item Multiplying $\ubf$ by some $a\in\Unit{p}$.
\end{itemize}

This defines a group action.
\footnote{
  For the interested reader, the group is isomorphic to the direct product of 
  $\Unit{p}$ with the $k$-dimensional hyperoctahedral group.
},
and hence the equivalence classes, i.e., the orbits, are well-defined. 
The first two actions are used in both the article by \cite{Rosenfeld} and \cite{Trakulthongchai},
but the third is new to this work. 
For completeness, we show that each of the actions above preserves eventual properness.

\begin{proposition}
  \label{prop:equivproper}
  Let $p$ be prime and $\ubf,\vbf\in\Int{p,1}^k$ be equivalent.
  If $\ubf$ is eventually $(k,p)$-proper, then so is $\vbf$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
  We only need to show that each of the above actions preserves eventual properness.
  For permuting it is trivial.
  Suppose $\ubf$ is eventually proper and $l$ is the integer for which every element in $\pi^{-1}(\ubf)$ is proper,
  where $\pi:=\pi_{lp\rightarrow p}$. Now let $\vbf'\in\pi(\vbf)$.

  If $\vbf=(u_1,\ldots,-u_i,\ldots,u_k)$, 
  then $\vbf'=(a_1p+u_1,\ldots,a_ip-u_i,\ldots,a_kp+u_k)$ for some integers $a_1,\ldots,a_k$. 
  In this case, pick 
  \[\ubf':=(a_1p+u_1,\ldots,(l-a_i)p+u_i,\ldots,a_kp+u_k)\in\pi^{-1}(\ubf).\]

  If $\vbf=a\ubf$ for some $a\in\Unit{p}$, 
  then by Chinese Remainder Theorem there is $b\in\Int{lp}$ such that $b\equiv a^{-1} \pmod{p}$ and $b\equiv1\pmod{l}$. 
  In particular, $b\in\Unit{lp}$ and 
  \[\ubf':=b\vbf'\in\pi^{-1}(\ubf)\]
  because $\pi(b\vbf')=\pi(b)\pi(\vbf')=a^{-1}(a\ubf)=\ubf$.

  Then, whichever way $\ubf'$ satisfies properness,
  it can be checked that $\vbf'$ also satisfies properness in the same way
  (we need $b$ to be a unit because if a time $t$ is a witness for $\ubf$, then the time $b^{-1}t$ is a witness for $\vbf$). 
  Therefore every speed tuple in $\pi(\vbf)$ is proper, and thus $\vbf$ is eventually proper.
\end{proof}

Therefore, we may only verify the eventual properness for a single element in an equivalence class. 
We choose the lexicographically smallest tuple as a representative of each equivalence class. 

\subsection{Verification algorithms}
\label{subsec:algo}
Recall from the established results that we need to find large sets of primes $P_k$ for each $k \in \set{10, 11, 12}$ 
and verify that $J(k, p) = \emptyset$ for each $p \in P_k$.

To do so, we gradually reduce upper bound of $J(k, p)$ by first choosing a large upper bound $S_1$ such that $J(k, p) \subset \pi_p S_1$ 
and uses strategies described in Section~\ref{sec:sieves} to form a successively better upper bound $S_n$ where the condition 
$J(k, p) \subset \pi_p S_n$ still holds.
We will use \emph{lifting diagrams} to explain the algorithm for each $k$.
In the diagrams, two kinds of arrows correspond to two actions from Section~\ref{sec:sieves}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $S_n\xrightarrow{\times c}S_{n+1}$ indicates lifting from $S_n$ to $S_{n+1}$ with constant $c$.
    \item $S_n\xrightarrow{\div d}S_{n+1}$ indicates projecting with $\pi_{dp\rightarrow p}$ (this is as same as $\pi_p$).
\end{itemize}
Since we only perform these two actions, Proposition~\ref{prop:liftproj} inductively asserts that 
$J(k,p) \subseteq \pi_p S_n$ for every $n$. 

From Propositions~\ref{prop:equivproper} and the inclusion $J(k, p) \subset I(k, p, 1)$, we may choose $S_1$ as the 
set of representatives of equivalence classes in $I(k, p, 1)$.

For $k=11$, since $k+1=12$ only has small prime factors $2$ and $3$, 
it is convenient to lift through its prime factors.
Hence, the lifting diagram is
\[S_1\xrightarrow{\times 2}S_2\xrightarrow{\times 2}S_3\xrightarrow{\times 2}S_4\xrightarrow{\times 2}S_5\xrightarrow{\div 16}S_6\xrightarrow{\times 3}S_7\xrightarrow{\times 3}S_8.\]
If $S_8$ is empty, we conclude that $J(k,p)\subseteq\pi_pS_8$ is empty.

For $k\in\{10,12\}$, $k+1$ is prime. By having only primes $p \ge k^2+k$ in the set $P_k$, 
Lemma~\ref{lem:corlarge} asserts that $\onetok \notin J(k, p)$, which can let us avoid the expensive $c = k+1$ lift
\footnote{
  In fact, we are able to use primes smaller than $k^2+k$ 
  by immitating the proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:resolvep} for the specific values of $p$ and $k$.
  We can also show emptiness of $J(10, p)$ for $p \in \set{103, 107, 109}$ and 
  $J(12, p)$ for $p \in \set{149, 156}$ using this method.
}.
Knowing this, we remove $\onetok$ from $S_1$ and use the following lifting diagram
\[S_1\xrightarrow{\times 2}S_2\xrightarrow{\times 2}S_3\xrightarrow{\times 2}S_4\xrightarrow{\div 8}S_5\xrightarrow{\times 3}S_6\xrightarrow{\times 3}S_7\xrightarrow{\div 9}S_8\]
which in particular does not contain a $c=11$ or $c=13$ lift.


\subsection{Further particulars}
We implement the algorithms in Subsection \ref{subsec:algo} in C++. 
Compared to the original implementations of \cite{rosenfeldcode} and \cite{tanupatcode},
we improve practical performance by refactoring to support parallel execution and by using more memory-efficient data structures.
The development of our code was assisted by AI-based tools.

In computing $S_1$, we modified the depth-first-search technique developed by \cite{rosenfeldcode} to account for the equivalent relation
mentioned in Subsection~\ref{subsec:equiv}. 
Notice that each representative tuple -- the lexicographically smallest among its equivalents -- has 1 as its first coordinate.
It therefore suffices to fix the first coordinate to 1 and start the search from the second coordinate.
The remaining coordinates are filled in using the same method used in \cite{Trakulthongchai}.
In particular, we check all tuples of integers $(v_1,\ldots,v_k)$ with 
\[1=v_1\leq v_2\leq\cdots\leq v_k\leq\frac{p-1}{2}.\] 

However, due to the interactions between permutations and independent sign changes, 
the implementation results in $k$ speed tuples per equivalence class,
so there is potential to improve the runtime by a further factor of $k$
However, we have not found a way to efficiently generate exactly one element per equivalence class

We should stress that this reduction is essential to obtaining our results. 
There are $p^k$ tuples in the $\Z^k_{p}$,
but with this reduction we only need to check a much smaller number, 
approximately \[\binom{p/2}{k-1}\approx\frac{p^k}{2^k(k-1)!}.\] 
Compared to the implementation of \cite{Rosenfeld} and \cite{Trakulthongchai}, 
we reduce the number of tuples to check by a factor of approximately $p/k$. 

These refinements dramatically improve runtime. We verified the case of $k=8$ in 4 seconds (15 minutes using \cite{tanupatcode}), 
$k=9$ in 90 seconds (23 hours using \cite{tanupatcode}), and $k=10$ in 45 minutes on a 10-core Apple M4 processor
(excluding compilation time). 
The proofs of $k=11$ and $k=12$ were run in multiple batches, with multiple versions, 
and on multiple machines, making the exact time taken incomparable to other cases. 

The source of the computation, along with the log files, can be found in the first author's GitHub repository \cite{mycode}.

\section{Concluding remarks}
\label{sec:further}
In Section \ref{sec:onetok}, we mentioned that, 
surprisingly, we only need to perform $c=2$ and $c=3$ lifts (each more than once) to eliminate all speed tuples apart
from those whose projection modulo $p$ is $\onetok$, which is a \emph{tight} speed tuple.
Tight speed tuples was defined by Goddyn and Wong \cite{goddyn2006tight} to be a speed tuple such that equality holds in
the statement of Conjecture \ref{lonelyconjred}. 
It is natural to ask whether the prominence of tight speed tuples in the sieving process is coincidental,
or whether a deeper structural explanation exists. 
In particular, one may ask whether a more systematic study of tight speed tuples could further accelerate the lifting process.

Looking ahead, the primary bottleneck in extending our results to $k = 13$ is the efficient computation of $I(k, p, 1)$. 
Progress here likely requires a finer structural understanding of improper speed tuples,
which could yield stronger pruning conditions and make the initial sieve more tractable.
